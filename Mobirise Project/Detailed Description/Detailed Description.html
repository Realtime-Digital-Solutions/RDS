Aims

Our group is shooting for the absolute best iteration of a product we think is viable in the modern world. We aim to assist the Health Care industry in creation of a device to ensure the survival of our species by helping to emit one of the many causes of heart failure. Firstly, we achieve this with our monitoring software that streamlines our data, direct to medical professionals who can make the best educational and logical decision on how to proceed. As you can see from the file “Landscape”, in the Overview section of this report, the current technology that our hospitals use today is the Holter monitor. From a technical standpoint, our overall aim is to achieve an eventual complete and polished product adhering to the Australian Regulatory Guidelines for Medical Devices (tga.gov.au) and working with manufacturers, to ensure we meet high quality standards. To achieve this, we have broken down our thought process into individual goals in the allocated time frame by assigning each team member as we also refer ourselves as, to a specific task. Here is a breakdown of the unified progress.

Goals

2 May 2021

Brainstorming

First on our agenda was the brainstorming session.
Each week our team met on Microsoft Teams, a group orientated video chat service with audio and text functionality which featured the ability to organise and share files and tasks among invited members. Shane had been working on a project that he believed was feasible within the time frame given his background and experience as an Electronics Engineer. As a patient covered by health care, Matthew offered insight into the medical industry and the technology they were using. Cameron, Declan, Rachel and Zachary, saw the potential in the idea, that it was interesting not only from a technological standpoint but from a necessary one. We achieved this goal by deliberating, “Who it should be aimed for” and “What can it do.” We believed this was the best route to go for and made our choice.

6 May 2021

Structure

After deciding on the project, we implemented a basic structure for the needs of the device. Such as hardware, software, technical and educational limitations. As we were working with time constraints, Shane applied his technical knowledge in sourcing the hardware and software to operate the device, including the communication protocol for relaying data to the analytical software. The rest of the group were assigned tasks to research the abilities of the hardware and software as well as self-educating ourselves in the programming languages that was going to run the backend. Data communication would be handled by
Wi-Fi, a wireless networking technology which is used for Local Area Networks and Internet services. The GUI (Graphical User Interface) would be made in
Qt Designer. We used Arduino, an open-source electronics software platform that allows communication between the electronic hardware by receiving inputs from the sensors to operate the functions it is programmed to do. The programming language chosen was Python, compiled in PyCharm, an IDE (Integrated Development Environment), to handle the Python Code. We chose Python because of its ease of use and features it offered suited our project. A minor iteration of the C programming language was handled by Shane, given his experience. We agreed and concluded on this.

9 May 2021

Task Allocation

We allocated each group member a Task which was created in Microsoft Teams, under the Task Tab. The tasks were created as theory-based writing, documented in Microsoft Word, reinforcing the technology that was going to be used and laying down the foundation of the project in written form. This was for presentation to potential customers and legal documentation of the project’s creation and existence. Our strengths were considered to make the overall decision as we have experience from many different fields of work. Collaboration was carried out on the areas that required group input, however any member that was experiencing difficulties, would be assisted. Our team had unique attributes and rarely did we see the need to require assistance other than the technical aspects of the device as some components were above our knowledge threshold and given the time frame to learn such intricacies were limited.

13 May 2021

Progress

Our group held a video conference each week on Thursday 8pm and Sunday 4pm AEST (Australian Eastern Standard Time). This was a progress update and report of the tasks set out from Microsoft Teams. Every member would express their thoughts, ideas, and difficulties they encountered during the process. We assisted each other with opinions from an educational and personal standpoint to assist any group member. All meetings were documented and an accounted for with Agendas and Notes taken, then posted in the Microsoft Teams wall post. This allowed everyone to remain updated on the latest file creations and version updates, as well as lifestyle changes.

16 May 2021

Prototype

A prototype of the device as seen in the Tools and Tech page of our Project Ideas Website was created. This gave us a 2D/3D model view of the hardware realised. This was Shane’s speciality and deciphered well ahead of schedule due to the known time constraints. The planning and progress that went into this was phenomenal and an attest to the skill and backed up by the group’s motivation and drive, saw us succeed by assisting one another based on our individual strengths. We decided to call our device “PulseBeat”, after the features that it offers. The ability to monitor the pulse to calculate heart beat rhythmical patterns, blood rate oxygen levels and if the patient has collapsed. The device could be worn of a part of the body that detects the human pulse.

20 May 2021

GUI

The GUI (Graphical User Interface) was created in Qt Designer. This was then converted into Python code for PyCharm to interpret the GUI into a working visual presentation. Our goal here was to take the static model of Qt Designer and forge it into a representation of what the medical personnel can visually understand and document. We implemented a new feature in this stage called Man-Down. If a patient’s heart rate dips below a certain level, an alarm will be triggered and sent out to medical personnel as data for them to analyse the situation. This is achieved by utilising an Arduino with an Accelerometer, connected to a heart rate sensor. This will measure the force of acceleration and positioning of the body to calculate whether the patient has suffered an attack, be it heart related or alternate source.

23 May 2021

Testing

Testing the communication variant of our device is particularly important to us. It shows that we have a working prototype to showcase to our customers. Any programming bugs, hardware or software errors will be addressed by troubleshooting the issue. It will be done in this stage to ensure all features are working as they should, and the device is transmitting data in a controlled environment to simulate a professional one.

28 May 2021

Beta Release

Beta release of Version 1.0 for feedback and analysis. This would include Human test volunteers. With their consent, our device will monitor and submit data in which medical professionals can analyse, under the Australian Department of Health’s approval and supervision. We feel that it is our exercised right to ensure the safety and well-being of people before final release.

Backup Plan

As with many projects, whether funded or unfunded, issues do sometimes arise. We plan to do as much as possible in the given time frame that we have been allocated. As a team and we think that we have achieved the best possible results based on that. The most important parts of the project are that we attained a working replica of the device that addresses many issues that the Traditional Holter Monitor poses. We have gathered all the necessary information to deem the project as feasible as we can, based on our statistical findings and research, with experience from a group member in the field as well as competitive products in the consumer market. Another important part of the project is the technical aspects to showcase our device in its infancy as a Beta release, provided we have worked out the bugs. Our priorities mainly lay in the creation of “PulseBeat”, as we believe that it has the potential to be something significant and an interest to outside parties. However, if we were to choose an aspect that should have priority over the rest, it would be the documentation of the device, explaining how the technology functions in the Backend aspect.
As mentioned, we were working with constraints to implement a working model and used the time as effectively as we could to achieve our goal. If we only had enough time or resources for one of our goals, we would allocate it to Research and Development. This is an important area where a vision is sometimes too complex and complicated for a team to achieve based on overall experience within the industry. These skills take time to enhance, and we must consider these limitations as well. Without proper planning and an intricate thought process, it would be building a project with a means to no end.
